How does a colorblind person see?

We see colors differently. About 1 in 20 people is colorblind in some way. This means that one or more types of cone cells in their eyes - the cells we use to detect colors - do not function normally. As a result, a colorblind person sees a reduced color space; two colors that appear distinct to a person with normal color vision may look identical to a colorblind person. This phenomenon is not random. Mathematically, there are lines across the color space - called "confusion lines" - such that every color on a confusion line appears identical to all other colors on the same confusion line for a colorblind person.

This means that we can, to some extent, simulate colorblindness. By mathematically reproducing the confusion line phenomenon, we can replace the colors of an image so that if two original colors are on the same confusion line, they are both replaced by the same new color. If done correctly, a person with normal color vision can experience a sort of colorblindness.

This website is aimed at people with normal or near-normal color vision. The goal of this site is to simulate for you what the color palette you have chosen looks like to colorblind people. The colors in the leftmost column are the "true" colors; they are displayed in the other three columns as they would appear to someone with protanopia, deuteranopia, or tritanopia, respectively.
